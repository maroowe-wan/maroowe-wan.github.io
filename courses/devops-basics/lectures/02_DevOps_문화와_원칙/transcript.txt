## Video 1: CALMS (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jiDwhKNS9A0)
Channel: Eficode

CALMS is one of the commonly used definitions of DevOps. It is the best single answer to the question: what is DevOps all about?

The C is for Culture. DevOps is about fostering a culture of continual learning and experimentation. This is done through shared responsibilities and breaking down silos. It is done by striving to create a psychologically safe environment where people are not afraid to bring their full self to work, and where bad news are learning opportunities rather than a reason for scolding.

In this psychologically safe setting of shared responsibilities, we invest in continuous improvement. We introduce tension in our system such that we always improve our current way of working.

The A is for Automation. This is the favorite part of DevOps for many people, as it is the most concrete and easily digestible part. Automation is about seeking out opportunities to eliminate manual work, in particular repetitive, tedious tasks that destroy the happiness of engineers. But it is also about making automation a first-class citizen of our software and our way of working.

This requires that we architect for automation. When our applications are difficult to test, run, or deploy automatically, we see that as a critical problem to solve, on par with feature delivery.

The L is for Lean. This means focusing on the flow through the entire value stream. It means empowering teams to work autonomously and independently deliver business value based on user feedback. It means working with WIP limits, small batch sizes, and continuously delivering small increments of working software to end users.

The M is for Measuring. We strive to build a data-driven culture based on measurements from our production environments and our processes. We can visualize these measurements to allow us to see trends and learn more about our product and organization. And finally, we use these measurements, our data, to drive our actions.

Sharing is about creating openness and transparency across the organization. Make incentives and goals visible. Sharing is also taking local solutions and wins and escalate them to the global organization, scaling the benefits vastly.

This has been DevOps explained with CALMS. I am Johann Abresco, a DevOps consultant at Eficode, a leading DevOps company in Europe.

## Video 2: DevOps Principles - The C.A.M.S. Model (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VySUutlo91E)
Channel: Coding Tech

DevOps is a relatively new practice. As the name suggests, DevOps consists of two parts: development and IT operations. We can think of DevOps as a natural extension of Agile because they are based on similar principles.

It aims to shorten the system's development life cycle and provide continuous delivery with high software quality. DevOps speeds up the process by means of developing and testing much smaller chunks of code and then deploying them continuously into production.

What makes DevOps great is that we can release more often, test more precisely, and receive feedback from production on the go. Eventually, we are delivering more value to our customers more often.

The main principles of DevOps are explained by the CAMS model, which was created by Damon Edwards and John Willis. CAMS stands for Culture, Automation, Measurement, and Sharing.

Principle 1: Culture

Even though we use some pretty advanced technology and tools in DevOps, at the core of what we're trying to solve are problems related to people and business. Culture is defined by the interaction of people in groups and is driven by behavior. Substantial communication improvement can result when there is a mutual understanding of others and their goals and responsibilities.

So in order to get all the benefits of DevOps, first we need to bridge the gap between development, quality assurance, and operations by setting the right culture. Traditional information technology business models split developers and IT operations into two distinct groups.

The DevOps culture principle, on the other hand, suggests having shared responsibilities between development and IT operations teams. This guarantees that the development team will be involved in software maintenance on the production while understanding deployment requirements better. At the same time, the IT operations team can have a better grasp on the functional needs of a system.

Another important aspect of the culture principle is that DevOps is centered around a business-oriented mindset of all team members. Basically, we are removing any silos and communication between the team members and stakeholders. That gives us a better understanding of business objectives and ensures that each team member takes personal responsibility for the product's success.

Changing the business culture by sharing responsibility and getting development and IT operations teams on the same page is a major goal of DevOps principle.

Principle 2: Automation

We all know that automation can save time, effort, and money. The automation principle focuses on people and processes, not just tools. The backbone of DevOps is the practices of continuous delivery and continuous integration.

This implies that we are constantly pushing the code to our testing and staging environments as well as deliver it to production. Unlike traditional deployment of large chunks of code, in DevOps we can deploy each time there is a production-ready bit of code.

Frequent updates make it vital to run tests faster and monitor code on production, which leads us to the general idea of automating the whole pipeline. In DevOps, this is accomplished by creating pipelines that perform code testing, integration, deployment, monitoring, and feedback exchange automatically.

Principle 3: Measurement

Using measurements will help in determining if progress is being made in the intended direction. The automation of a feedback loop provides valuable insights about the features deployed on production. As we are dealing with smaller chunks of code, the culprits are much easier to spot and fix.

The DevOps measurement principle encourages you to see the forest from the trees by taking a look at the entire operation and assessing it as a whole, and not just focusing on small parts.

DevOps teams should develop and track certain key metrics in order to help check their systems. These metrics can include things like: lead time (the time it takes to implement, test, and deploy code), deployment frequency, change failure rate, average time to recovery, average time between failures, and defect escape rate.

Overall, by measuring various key performance indicators, the team should strive to continuously improve their systems and IT operations.

Principle 4: Sharing

Sharing is caring. This phrase explains the DevOps philosophy better than anything else, as it highlights the importance of collaboration. It is crucial to share feedback, best practices, and knowledge among teams, since this promotes transparency, creates collective intelligence, and eliminates constraints.

DevOps processes, similar to Agile and Scrum, place a very high premium on transparency and openness. When everyone on the team is open, this helps in building trust and a fun working environment. Transparency ensures that every team member can see and measure the progress and work towards a common goal.

Besides spreading knowledge, it helps to tighten feedback loops and enables the organization to continuously improve. This collective intelligence makes the team a more efficient unit and allows it to become greater than just the sum of its parts.

Given the CAMS model, it is now clear that the pure automation component of the build and delivery chain is only a small part of DevOps. In reality, DevOps is not just an automation tool, but way more than that. It is a culture and a state of mind which uses the same concepts as agility and lean principles.

## Video 3: DevOps CALMS Framework (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LdMtHOWhchk)
Channel: Jayaprakash Balakrishnan

In this video, we will talk about DevOps frameworks. There is a popular framework called the CALMS framework. Just Humble is the one who pioneered this framework.

What does a framework do? A framework helps in organizing the activities. It gives a design or a guideline document based on which you can draft all your steps. It's like a road map. But what matters is there are so many maps in the world. It is important that you take that map and follow the steps. That's when it will take us to the destination.

We will speak in detail about this CALMS framework and what CALMS stands for: Culture, Automation, Lean, Measurements, and then Sharing.

Culture

Culture is often been talked about from various aspects, and everyone talks about culture. Let's see the details of what it actually means:

First: From a fear of failure to a fail-fast, fail-forward approach. Failing is taken up very supportively and in a balanced manner. If you continue to fail, you probably won't beat the deliverables, but experimentation and then failing is an important part of the culture. This can be driven by leaders and how we act whenever failure happens. When someone breaks something, it's about how we react.

Second: From tech obsession to customer obsession. This always happens, especially in startups. We build up a great product, but there may not be any customers, and we may be building a product which is not the customer's pain point. It is very important that we move from a tech obsession to customer obsessions. We put ourselves in the customer's shoes. Customer stories are very important. Also, sometimes the "jobs to be done" kind of frameworks can help here. What is the actual job which the customer wants to get done?

Third: From organizational silos to a collaborative model. This is one of the important aspects of DevOps. We want to break all the silos. Previously, it used to be dev and ops in separate streams. In case of any issues, there used to be a war-like situation. Then we integrated with the business, calling it Business DevOps. Now we are integrating security, saying DevSecOps. It is important that teams working together is highly acknowledged. As much as possible, try to break organizational silos. That's very important.

Fourth: From big data confusion to real-time, information-driven insights. There is so much data we collect and so many queries being written, and a lot of work happens there. There is a lot of big data platforms out there, and what is important is that time-driven insights. Most of the time, you will be able to get these things by going into their field and talking to the customers.

Automation

There is a notion that speed can sometimes be dangerous. Why do we automate? We want speed. We want to get it done faster. It is important to choose which activity has to be done faster and which activity has to be done in the spirit of things.

Just by throwing technology or some tools onto some processes, it is not going to work. It is important to look at where the speed and the agility has to be brought in. Choosing the right processes is important, and also it has to be calibrated over a period of time through iteration.

Automation success isn't about the tools but the people and process. This is obvious because often it would be like: get that tool, implement the tool, buy a product and implement it. That's not going to solve the problem. We have to get to the root of the issues and then address it. People and processes need to be set in such a way that automation becomes a success.

It is more than just infrastructure and application automation. There are so many tools available. The ecosystem is huge with new tools coming in all the time. There are specific focus tools coming in for application automation and infrastructure automation. On the other side, all these behemoths (AWS, Azure, Google) are coming up with their own tools and products.

What is important is to know what are all the exact processes which need to be automated. Go beyond that and whether it really solves the problem and impacts the customer. That's where the benefits of automation will be able to be realized.

Lean Methods

There are various aspects of lean methods: reduce wastage, avoid redoing things, and so on. Look at implementing them. Keep the processes lean, slim, and that's when it will be repeatable and scalable. If there are thicker processes, probably people may not follow that. That's another important aspect of DevOps: simplification, leaner and then meaner methods, repeatable processes.

Measurement

How simple: two, three measurements across all the levels. It should give the holistic aspect of it.

First: People measurements. What are people measurements? The job satisfaction is very important. Second, whether people enjoy working in the teams. Do they feel challenged and motivated to move to the next level? That is very important. They should also feel that it is doable. The goals are achievable. That's another important aspect of DevOps. Rather than having a very big goal which you won't be able to see after three months or six months, having goals that are two weeks away so you're going to see what is going to happen, whether it is working or not. That is the satisfaction and the excitement that is important. That is one of the people measurements.

Second: Process measurements. How well the process is working? Is it really helping us? Is it really adding value? This is where it is important to ask some tough questions. What if I remove that process? What will be the outcome of it? These kinds of tough questions need to be asked. And then the measurement has to be there. How much of the process helps in the improvement? That's where process measurements come into picture.

Third: Technology measurements. What is the mean time to repair? How long does it sustain? What kind of efficiency does this tool bring in? How much time gets reduced? What time it takes? How much of the improvement it produces? These are all the technology measurements which are important for DevOps success.

Sharing

This is easier said than it is important that sharing across all the levels happens. The VPs should be able to communicate to each other. That's when the developer will start communicating too. Often, the barriers are created not at the lower level but at the upper level. The feedback and feed-forward mechanism has to keep going. That is one of the important aspects of the infinite, endless loop of DevOps. There is a continuous feedback, continuous integration, and continuous delivery in all the steps across all of DevOps.

Summary

Keep CALMS and do DevOps. CALMS stands for Culture, Automation, Lean, Measurements, and then Sharing.

In Culture: Move from a fear of failure to a fail-fast, fail-forward approach. Encourage people to fail. That's when new innovations will come. Tech obsession to customer obsessions. Focus on the customer. Whether he is happy. Solve his problems, not some very good-looking, highly functional product. Organizational silos to collaborative model. Work in collaboration. Sharing is very important rather than having it siloed.

Big data confusion to real-time, information-driven insights. Insights is what is important, not some complex data analysis.

Next is Automation: Speed can be dangerous at times, and speed is not to be recommended always. Keep that as a guiding principle of automation. Automation isn't all about tools but people and processes. People and processes have to be robust. Processes need to capture the failures. There can be a feedback mechanism. That is about automation. It's also more than just infrastructure and application automation. There are more steps to build efficiency, and it is important that you focus on them.

Lean methods: Keep it leaner and meaner and simple and sustainable processes.

Measurements: People measurements like job satisfactions and whether they enjoy challenges and are very comfortable in working with the team and sharing some of their failures. Process measurements: how important and how impactful the processes are. Are these robust? In case if one of the things fails, is it able to capture it? That is important. Next is technology measurements like mean time to repair. The efficiency is brought in those aspects of it.

The last one is Sharing: Caring. There has to be some good feedback and feed-forward mechanism so that we are able to implement all these processes and communication flows freely.

Hope this CALMS framework was useful. This is one of the important frameworks which should help you to implement the DevOps principles so that they are hands-on and effective.
