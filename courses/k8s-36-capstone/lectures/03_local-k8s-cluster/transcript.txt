Troubleshooting tips
Don't get confused when&nbsp;&nbsp;
you have 5 pods and you delete one, then&nbsp;
you run kubectl get pods and you see 5&nbsp;&nbsp;
pods while you expect 4. Here is the thing:&nbsp;
When you set the number of replicas to 5:&nbsp;
Kubernetes ensures that 5 replicas&nbsp;
(pods) of your application are running&nbsp;&nbsp;
at all times. This is the desired state that&nbsp;
you declared for your deployment. If a pod&nbsp;&nbsp;
is deleted (intentionally or due to failure),&nbsp;
Kubernetes' self-healing mechanism kicks in to&nbsp;&nbsp;
restore the desired state by creating a new pod&nbsp;
to maintain the specified number of replicas.&nbsp;
When you manually delete a pod using:
kubectl delete pod &lt;pod_name&gt;&nbsp;
Kubernetes notices that the number of running&nbsp;
replicas dropped below the desired count of 5.&nbsp;&nbsp;
To maintain the desired state, it automatically&nbsp;
creates a new pod to replace the one you deleted.
This is how Kubernetes works in this&nbsp;case:
Desired State: You set the deployment&nbsp;&nbsp;
to have 5 replicas (i.e., 5 pods).
Current State: When you delete a pod,&nbsp;&nbsp;
Kubernetes sees that only 4 pods are running.
Reconciliation: Kubernetes continuously monitors&nbsp;&nbsp;
the cluster and detects that the current state (4&nbsp;
running pods) does not match the desired state (5&nbsp;&nbsp;
replicas). It immediately schedules a&nbsp;
new pod to bring the total back to 5.
This self-healing behavior is one of the key&nbsp;
benefits of Kubernetes. It ensures that your&nbsp;&nbsp;
applications maintain high availability&nbsp;
and recover from failures automatically.&nbsp;
kubectl describe &amp; kubectl&nbsp;logs&nbsp;
Sometimes Pods can get stuck in weird&nbsp;
states like Pending or CrashLoopBackOff.&nbsp;&nbsp;
Use kubectl describe pod &lt;pod-name&gt; to dig&nbsp;
deeper and understand what's going wrong.&nbsp;
You can also check the logs with kubectl logs&nbsp;
&lt;pod-name&gt; to get more detailed error messages.
